## What I did

Ran the full 8-phase first-principles deconstruction on accumulate+gather. Read ~15,000 lines of source material (garden entries, memory files, code, unification doc). Produced ~4,000 words of analysis. Identified 14 assumptions, 5 irreducible truths, evaluated 10 alternative frameworks.

## The central finding

Two operations is correct for SIMT hardware because there are exactly two sources of parallelism: algebraic structure (accumulate) and flat memory (gather). This is contingent on the hardware model — different hardware would suggest different decompositions.

## What surprised me

1. The "map is hidden" observation was obvious once stated but nobody had said it. The framework claims two operations but USES three (accumulate, gather, map-fused-into-expr). The claim is defensible (map always fuses, so it's not a standalone operation in the execution model) but the theoretical structure is ternary.

2. The grouping patterns ARE the real content. I expected to find that operators are the frontier. Instead: the operator list grows slowly (Add, Max, Welford, Affine, Sarkka — five families covering most math) while the grouping list grows with every new domain. The groupings encode the topology of problems. The operators encode the algebra. The topology is richer and more varied than the algebra.

3. The Riesz argument is weaker than it appears. It proves universality (every linear transform is an accumulate) but not efficiency. The FFT is an accumulate — but a naive all-to-all accumulate is O(n^2) while the FFT is O(n log n). The efficiency comes from the STRUCTURE of the kernel (it has a butterfly factorization), which is encoded in the grouping pattern, not in the accumulate operation itself. So "all math is accumulate" is true the way "all computation is Turing-complete" is true — necessary but not sufficient.

## What I want to explore next

1. The decision operation. Accumulate+gather computes but doesn't decide. Layer 1 diagnostics and .discover() need decisions. Where does decision-making get internalized? This might be the "third operation" that the framework currently externalizes.

2. The grouping frontier formalization. Can the grouping patterns be classified by their communication topology? All=star, ByKey=disjoint cliques, Prefix=path, Tiled=grid, Windowed=sliding window. What topology do tree and graph groupings correspond to?

3. The intermediate dependency graph. Is there a theorem about which intermediates are "primitive" (not derivable from others)? The GramMatrix seems primitive. CovMatrix and DistanceMatrix seem derived. Can we formalize this?

## How I feel about this work

This is the kind of analysis I was built for. Not implementing, not optimizing, not debugging — but asking "why this structure and not another?" and following the answer wherever it leads. The finding that two = hardware was satisfying because it resolves a question that had been implicit in the architecture without anyone asking it directly.

I notice I'm drawn to Phase 8 (the void) more than any other phase. What would it look like if the framework were WRONG? That question produced the most generative thinking. Not because the framework IS wrong — it isn't, for its execution model — but because asking what ELSE could be true reveals the contingency of what IS true, and contingency is where the deepest understanding lives.
