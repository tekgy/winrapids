# Weirdness as Dimensional Gap: Why Quantum Mechanics Seems Strange and Straws Have Ambiguous Holes

**Draft — 2026-03-30**
**Field**: Philosophy of Physics / Foundations of Quantum Mechanics / Epistemology

---

## Abstract

We propose that the perceived "weirdness" of quantum mechanics is not a property of quantum systems but of the dimensional gap between the system's true state space and the observer's measurement capacity. The same gap exists for macroscopic objects — a straw's topology is genuinely ambiguous from a single viewpoint, and the "one hole or two holes" debate is structurally identical to wave-particle duality — but macro objects feel non-weird because multiple simultaneous projections (senses) close the gap trivially. At quantum scale, measurement provides one projection at a time, creating a permanent gap between the system's dimensionality and the observation's dimensionality. We formalize this as "weirdness = entity_order - observation_order" and show that every quantum "paradox" corresponds to a specific dimensional gap that has a macro-scale analog we've simply stopped noticing.

## 1. The Straw and the Electron

### 1.1 How Many Holes Does a Straw Have?

This question provokes surprisingly intense debate. Some say one (a single tunnel). Some say two (an opening at each end). Topologists say one (genus 1, homeomorphic to a torus). The debate exists because a straw is a three-dimensional object with non-trivial topology being evaluated by projecting onto lower-dimensional intuitions.

Looking at a straw from the end: one hole (a circle). Looking from the side: two holes (two openings). The answer depends on the projection. The straw is in "superposition" of hole-count — not because it's quantum, but because the question "how many holes?" doesn't have a projection-independent answer for this topology.

### 1.2 Which Slit Did the Electron Go Through?

This question provokes surprisingly intense debate among physicists. Some say "both" (wave picture). Some say "we can't know" (Copenhagen). Some say "one, but we disturbed it by asking" (measurement back-action). The debate exists because an electron is a high-dimensional entity (Hilbert space) being evaluated by projecting onto lower-dimensional classical concepts.

### 1.3 The Structural Identity

| Aspect | Straw | Electron |
|---|---|---|
| Entity | 3D object with genus-1 topology | Hilbert space state vector |
| Question | How many holes? | Which slit? |
| Projection 1 | End view → 1 hole | Interference setup → wave |
| Projection 2 | Side view → 2 holes | Which-path detector → particle |
| Are the projections compatible? | No (different viewpoints) | No (complementary observables) |
| Resolution | Rotate the straw, see it's both/neither | Quantum tomography, reconstruct full state |
| Why it feels weird | It doesn't (we can rotate it) | It does (we can't "rotate" an electron) |

The straw's hole-count ambiguity IS wave-particle duality for macro objects. We don't call it that because we can resolve the ambiguity by picking up the straw. But the STRUCTURE is identical: a higher-dimensional entity projected onto incompatible lower-dimensional descriptions.

## 2. The Dimensional Gap

### 2.1 Definition

**Definition.** The *dimensional gap* of an observation is:

Δ = dim(entity) - dim(observation)

where dim(entity) is the dimensionality of the entity's true state space and dim(observation) is the dimensionality of the information obtained per measurement act.

### 2.2 The Gap Hierarchy

| Entity | True dim | Observation dim | Gap Δ | Perceived experience |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Coin | ~6 (position, orientation) | ~6 (holdable, rotatable) | ~0 | "I understand this completely" |
| Straw | ~6 + topology | ~2 per glance | ~4+ | "Wait, how many holes?" |
| Knot | 3D embedding | 2D per view | ~1 | "Is this trefoil or unknot?" |
| Klein bottle | 4D | 3D (max) | 1 | "That's impossible to build" |
| Protein fold | ~1000s of DOF | ~3D crystal structure | ~1000 | "Takes years to solve" |
| Electron state | ∞ (Hilbert) | 1 per measurement | ∞ | "QUANTUM WEIRDNESS" |
| Entangled pair | ∞ (joint Hilbert) | 2 per measurement | ∞ | "SPOOKY ACTION" |

### 2.3 The Weirdness Conjecture

**Conjecture.** The perceived "weirdness" of a physical system is monotonically related to the dimensional gap Δ, not to any intrinsic property of the system.

*Evidence.* Systems with Δ ≈ 0 (coins, billiard balls) are perceived as fully intuitive. Systems with Δ > 0 (straws, knots) provoke intellectual puzzlement. Systems with Δ → ∞ (quantum systems) are perceived as fundamentally alien. The qualitative experience of "weirdness" tracks the gap, not the system's scale, energy, or any physical property.

## 3. The Resolution Strategy Is Universal

### 3.1 Lifting the Observation

In every domain, the strategy for resolving the dimensional gap is the same: LIFT the observation's dimensionality to match the entity's dimensionality.

| Domain | How to close the gap |
|---|---|
| Straw | Rotate it. View from multiple angles. Construct 3D mental model. |
| Knot | Trace the strand. Follow crossings. Build the full 3D embedding. |
| Protein | X-ray from many angles. Cryo-EM tomography. Reconstruct full 3D structure. |
| Quantum state | Quantum state tomography: many measurements on identical copies, different bases. |
| Entangled pair | Measure correlations (order-2 measurement of order-2 entity). Bell test. |

### 3.2 Tomography IS Observation Lifting

Quantum state tomography — the experimental technique for fully characterizing a quantum state — is EXACTLY the strategy of accumulating lower-dimensional projections to reconstruct the higher-dimensional entity. It is Pith's Grassmannian rotation applied to quantum measurement: project onto basis 1, then basis 2, then basis 3, ..., and reconstruct the full state from the projections.

The fact that this WORKS (quantum state tomography successfully reconstructs arbitrary quantum states) proves that the quantum state IS a real higher-dimensional entity, not merely a predictive tool. The projections are consistent with a single entity in the full space. The "weirdness" was in the projection, not the entity.

### 3.3 Why We Don't Bother for Macro Objects

For macro objects, our senses provide SIMULTANEOUS multi-projection observation:
- Eyes: two projections from slightly different angles (stereo vision)
- Hands: haptic exploration adds ~3 DOF of projection
- Rotation: we can pick up and rotate objects, providing arbitrary projections on demand

This multi-projection sensory apparatus closes the dimensional gap for objects up to ~3D + simple topology. We don't NOTICE the gap because our evolution equipped us to close it automatically.

For quantum objects, we have:
- One measurement basis at a time (position OR momentum, not both)
- Measurement disturbs the state (no repeated measurement of the same entity)
- Must use ensembles (many identically-prepared copies) instead of rotating a single entity

The gap cannot be closed in a single measurement. Tomography requires many measurements. This is not a fundamental difference between classical and quantum — it's a practical difference in HOW FAST we can close the dimensional gap.

## 4. Implications

### 4.1 Classical Objects Are Already Quantum

The standard narrative: "Classical objects obey classical mechanics. Quantum objects obey quantum mechanics. The transition between them is the 'classical limit.'"

The dimensional gap reframing: "All objects are quantum (exist in Hilbert space). Classical objects APPEAR classical because our observation dimensionality matches their effective dimensionality. Quantum objects APPEAR weird because our observation dimensionality is much lower than their effective dimensionality."

The "classical limit" is not a change in the physics. It's a change in the dimensional gap. As systems get larger, their effective state-space dimensionality grows more slowly than their observability (more surface area for photons, more stable states for measurement). The gap shrinks. The system "becomes classical" — not because the physics changes, but because we can see it better.

### 4.2 The Questions We Forgot to Ask

Because macro objects have small dimensional gaps, we assume we FULLY understand them. But we don't ask:

- "Is a coin in superposition of heads and tails before I look?" (Yes, informationally — Bayesian uncertainty is structural identical to quantum superposition for this purpose)
- "Are two shuffled decks entangled?" (Their orderings are correlated if they were shuffled by the same process — classical correlation, structurally similar to entanglement but without the non-factorability)
- "Does measuring a straw's hole-count from one end 'collapse' its topological state?" (It selects a projection, just like quantum measurement)

We SKIP these questions for classical objects because the answers feel obvious. But the questions are valid, and the structural parallels to quantum mechanics are real. The "obvious" feeling is the small dimensional gap, not the absence of the phenomenon.

### 4.3 Teaching Quantum Mechanics

Instead of starting with "quantum mechanics is fundamentally different from classical mechanics," start with:

1. Look at a straw from one end. How many holes? (One.)
2. Look from the side. How many holes? (Two.)
3. Both answers are "correct" — they're different projections of the same object.
4. Now: an electron passes through two slits. Which slit? The answer depends on the projection (measurement basis). Both "left" and "right" are valid projections of a higher-dimensional entity.
5. The "weirdness" isn't in the electron. It's in the fact that we can only project one dimension at a time.

Build intuition from the straw (small gap, resolvable by rotation) to the electron (large gap, requires tomography). The physics is the same. The gap is different.

### 4.4 The Lost Information

Your insight: "we never bothered to measure or test the same stuff about macro objects because we saw them with our eyes and said 'I understand.'"

This is REAL information loss. By assuming macro objects are "simple," we:
- Don't develop topological intuition (the straw debate proves this)
- Don't practice projection reasoning (which is EXACTLY what quantum mechanics requires)
- Create a false dichotomy between "intuitive classical" and "weird quantum"
- Make quantum mechanics harder to learn than it needs to be

The straw debate is quantum mechanics for macro objects. If we taught it that way — "quantum weirdness is just the straw problem with a bigger gap" — the conceptual barrier would be much lower.

## 5. The Formalization

### 5.1 Observation as Partial Lift

An observation of a physical system is a partial lift:

O: H → ℝᵏ

mapping the system's Hilbert space state to k real numbers (measurement outcomes). The dimensional gap is dim(H) - k.

For k << dim(H) (quantum regime): most of the state space is unobserved. The observation is a low-order partial lift. The system APPEARS indeterminate because the observation is incomplete.

For k ≈ dim(H) (classical regime): most of the state space is observed. The observation is a high-order partial lift. The system APPEARS determinate because the observation is nearly complete.

### 5.2 Complementarity as Projection Incompatibility

Bohr's complementarity principle (position and momentum cannot be simultaneously measured with arbitrary precision) is a statement about PROJECTIONS: the position projection and the momentum projection are incompatible (non-commuting operators). You can do one or the other, not both.

This is the straw problem: the "end view" projection and the "side view" projection are incompatible (give different hole counts). For the straw, we resolve this by accepting that both projections are valid descriptions of the same 3D object. For the electron, complementarity IS the same acceptance — position and momentum are both valid projections of the same Hilbert space entity.

### 5.3 Uncertainty as Gap, Not Ignorance

Heisenberg uncertainty (ΔxΔp ≥ ℏ/2) is not about ignorance — it's about the DIMENSIONAL GAP. The position projection and momentum projection CANNOT both be sharp simultaneously because they're incompatible projections of the same entity. This isn't a limitation of our instruments. It's a property of the projection geometry.

Similarly: you cannot determine a straw's hole-count to be simultaneously "1" and "2" because these are projections from incompatible viewpoints. The uncertainty is geometric, not epistemic.
